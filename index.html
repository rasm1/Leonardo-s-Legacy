Leonardo's Legacy

A look into the works of the genius himself, Leonardo da Vinci

Introduction

Leonardo da Vinci is one of history's most celebrated polymaths, known for his remarkable contributions to art, science, engineering, and anatomy during the Italian Renaissance. His innovative approach to the arts and science have caused him to become a leading figure in the fields of painting, sculpture, engineering, and invention.
Leonardo was more than just an artistic genius, his work in fields such as anatomy and engineering have laid the groundwork for future scientific advancements. He meticulously studied human and animal anatomy, creating detailed sketches that were centuries ahead of their time. His engineering concepts, such as flying machines and war devices, showcase his inventive spirit and visionary thinking. Leonardo da Vinci embodies the quintessential "Renaissance Man," a term used to describe individuals with wide-ranging interests and expertise. His legacy endures through his art, scientific investigations, and the inspiration he continues to provide to artists, scientists, and thinkers across the globe.

early life

Leonardo da Vinci was born on April 15th, 1452, in Vinci, a rural village in Tuscany, Italy. His full name was Leonardo di ser Piero da Vinci, which indicates to us that he was the illegitimate son of Piero da Vinci, a notary, and Caterina, a woman of the lower class.
Despite being an illegitimate, Leonardo was raised in his father's household, where he received an informal education in reading, writing, and arithmetic. Growing up in rural Vinci, Leonardo was surrounded by the beauty of nature, which likely formed his interests and enhanced is oberservational skills.
He spent much of his youth exploring the countryside, examining plants, animals, and the environment, learning as much as he could in the process. This early exposure to the natural world later became a significant influence on his work in both art and science.
At the age of 14, Leonardo moved to Florence, where he began his apprenticeship with the renowned artist Andrea del Verrocchio, an important step in his artistic development. Under Verrocchio's guidance, Leonardo learned various artistic techniques and crafts, such as painting, sculpture, metalworking, and drafting. During this apprenticeship, he also honed his observational skills further and developed a deep appreciation for the beauty of arts and sciences.
By his early 20s, Leonardo's talent was becoming apparent, and he began receiving commissions for his own works. This early period of his life laid the foundation for his later achievements, as he combined his artistic skills with his passion for science, engineering, and anatomy. These formative years in Florence set the stage for Leonardo da Vinci's emergence as one of the most versatile and innovative minds of the Italian Renaissance.

noteworthy creations

The Mona Lisa

The Mona Lisa, perhaps the most famous artwork ever created. The Mona Lisa was painted approximately painted between the years of 1503 & 1506 and The painting is supossedly a portrait of Lisa Gherardini, the 3rd wife of the client Francesco del Giocondo. The Mona Lisa is an oil painting made using poplarwood. Much is left unknown about this masterpiece, yet this is one of the only artworks where historians are sure that it was painted by the likes of Leonardo da Vinci himself.

The Last Supper

The Last supper is a mural painting created by da Vinci, made in the years of 1495- 1498, housed in the refectory of the convent of Santa Maria delle Grazie in Milan. The painting represents the last Supper of jesus and his 12 apostles as told in the Gospel of John, where jesus tells his apostles that one of them will betray him. The painting combines a miriad of considerations to produce a masterpiece. This artpiece clearly displays da Vinci's mastery over elements such as handling of space, displays of human emotion and treatment of motion to produce what you see in front of you today. Recognised by even the most astute artists, the last supper has gone into history as one of the great works of man.

The Vetruvian Man

This is another one of Leonardo da Vinci’s famous works and exists in ink and paper. This drawing depicts a man in two superimposed positions. While the arms and legs are straight in one, the second image shows it to be held apart. Both are inscribed in a square and circle. This painting was also accompanied by notes based on Roman Architect Vitruvius’s works, where it gets its name from. This painting mainly demonstrates Leonardo’s depiction of the ideal proportions of the human body. While the inscription in a circle and square comes from the description in Vitruvius’s book, Leonardo did not follow the arm measurements mentioned in the same book. He included the ones that he found by measuring male models in Milan. This is surely one of the most famous paintings of Leonardo da Vinci.
